Research Interests

My research interests encompass two primary areas: computational sustainability and human-computer interaction. I have had the privilege to engage in various research experiences, and below are some select examples of my contributions in these fields.


Verizon: Identifying Objects and Sorting by Color

Over 5 months, I collaborated closely with Verizon engineers and data scientists on a project aimed at training object detection models for product classification. My contributions involved creating training and validation datasets by annotating images of popular phone models and developing a color sorting algorithm by extracting RGB values from these images. These datasets played a crucial role in training the object detection models, which achieved remarkable results in sorting phones by brand and color with a high confidence level of 96% and a precision of 98%. Additionally, the color sorting algorithm was capable of accurately sorting and predicting over 800 distinct colors.

Featured Projects

Below are a select few of my most challenging projects that required a fair bit of research.


Flick Pick: A Collaborative Filtering-Based Movie Recommendation Chrome Extension

Flick Picks is a movie recommendation Chrome extension that utilizes collaborative filtering to create personalized movie suggestions based on user preferences and the ratings of a community of users.

Super Mario for VGA

In this project, I developed a platformer game inspired by the 8-bit Super Mario for VGA using Verilog and Vivado. The goal was to create a game where the player could move through a map with obstacles and enemies, collect coins, and reach the end. I designed various mechanics, including obstacles with unique behaviors, collision checks, and gravity for realistic jumping and falling.

Electric Guitar with Notch-Filter for Signal Attenuation

In this project, I successfully built an electric guitar and designed a band-reject filter using a simple op-amp circuit to amplify the guitar pickup's electrical signal and reject specific frequencies. The project consisted of three main phases. In the first phase, I constructed the guitar using a wooden block as the neck and a cigar box as the body, with a Seymour Duncan pickup. In the second phase, I designed the band-reject filter to reject a selected frequency of 196 Hz. I experimented with different resistor and capacitor values using LTSpice and then built the circuit accordingly. In the third phase, I physically built the notch filter and connected it to the guitar pickup, producing sound from the guitar.

Raspberry PI-Based K-Means for Music Genre Classification

In this project, I contributed to creating an IoT system that utilized K-means clustering, connecting a Raspberry Pi with RESTFUL APIs to recommend music based on sound sensor data. The process involved using sound sensors to collect data, which was then subjected to K-means clustering to determine the genre of the music. This genre information was used to search the top three tracks belonging to that genre which are displayed on the Raspberry Pi's LCD.
